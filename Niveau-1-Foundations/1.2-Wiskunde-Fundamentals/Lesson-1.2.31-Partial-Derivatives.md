# Lesson 1.2.31: Partial Derivatives

**Estimated Time**: 8 hours | **Difficulty**: Intermediate  
**Prerequisites**: Lesson 1.2.30 | **Topic**: Calculus for ML

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Define partial derivatives
- Interpret change along one axis
- Compute simple partials
- Connect partials to gradients

---

## Core Ideas

- Hold other variables constant
- Partial derivative is directional change along one axis

---

## Exercises

### Difficulty 1: Starter
- Exercise 1.2.31.1: Define partial derivative in one sentence.
- Exercise 1.2.31.2: Compute partial of f(x,y)=x^2+y^2 with respect to x.
- Exercise 1.2.31.3: Explain holding variables constant.

### Difficulty 2: Intermediate
- Exercise 1.2.31.4: Compute partials for f(x,y)=xy+2x.
- Exercise 1.2.31.5: Interpret partials at a point.
- Exercise 1.2.31.6: Explain partials in a surface plot.

### Difficulty 3: Advanced
- Exercise 1.2.31.7: Compute partials for a 3-variable function.
- Exercise 1.2.31.8: Explain how partials form gradients.
- Exercise 1.2.31.9: Use partials to find critical points.

## Quiz & Assessment

### Quick Check (True/False)
1. Partial derivatives hold other variables fixed. (True)
2. Partial derivatives are only for 1D functions. (False)
3. Gradients are built from partials. (True)
4. Partials describe local slope. (True)

### Conceptual Questions
5. Why do we need partial derivatives in ML?
6. How do partials differ from total derivatives?
7. What is a critical point?

### Coding Challenge
8. Compute partials of f(x,y)=x^2+xy.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Partials measure change in one variable
- Holding others constant is essential
- Gradients collect partials
- Partials drive multivariable optimization
